Epidermolysis Bullosa 表皮分解性水泡症

Epidermolysis Bullosa (EB) is a rare genetic disorder that causes the skin to be extremely fragile and prone to blistering and tearing with minor friction or trauma. It is primarily caused by mutations in genes responsible for skin integrity, affecting proteins that hold skin layers together. EB is usually inherited and presents in several forms, with varying severity. EB’s Symptoms include blistering skin, open wounds, pain, and scarring. In severe cases, internal linings, such as the mouth and esophagus, may also be affected. Complications can include infections, malnutrition, and increased risk of skin cancer. EB affects about 20 every 1 million live births globally, impacting individuals of all races and genders. The diseases occur worldwide, with no specific geographic concentration, though access to treatment may vary. There is currently no cure for EB. Treatment focuses on managing symptoms, preventing infections, and protecting the skin. This includes wound care, pain management, and nutritional support. Some therapies, such as gene therapy and stem cell treatments, have been approved for treatment of EB, but the existing treatment options are all very expensive.

Bullous Pemphigoid 大疱性類天疱瘡

Bullous Pemphigoid is a rare autoimmune skin disorder that causes large, fluid-filled blisters (bullae) on areas of the skin that flex, such as the lower abdomen, upper thighs, or armpits. It occurs when the immune system mistakenly attacks the layer of tissue between the epidermis (outer skin layer) and the dermis (underlying skin layer), causing inflammation and blistering. The exact cause is unknown, but Bullous Pemphigoid is linked to an overactive immune response, where antibodies attack proteins in the skin. Factors like certain medications (e.g., loop diuretics, antibiotics), UV light, or radiation therapy can sometimes trigger the condition. Symptoms: Symptoms include red, itchy rashes that evolve into large blisters filled with clear or yellow fluid. The blisters are often painful and may rupture, leading to crusting and open sores. Bullous Pemphigoid primarily affects older adults, typically over the age of 60, and occurs equally in men and women. It is more common in Western populations, but it can affect individuals worldwide. Existing treatment methods aim to reduce inflammation, relieve itching, and prevent blister formation. Common therapies include corticosteroids (topical or oral), immunosuppressive drugs, and anti-inflammatory medications. Managing the condition can often lead to long-term remission, but ongoing medical care is typically required to prevent relapses. Early diagnosis and treatment can improve outcomes and help prevent complications.

Pulmonary Hypertension 肺高血壓

Pulmonary Hypertension (PH) is a condition characterized by high blood pressure in the pulmonary arteries, which transport blood from the heart to the lungs. This elevated pressure occurs when the small blood vessels in the lungs become narrowed, blocked, or damaged, causing the heart to work harder to pump blood through the lungs. Over time, this strain can lead to heart failure. Pulmonary hypertension can arise from various underlying conditions, including heart disease, lung disease (such as COPD or interstitial lung disease), chronic blood clots, and genetic predisposition. It can also develop without a clear cause, known as idiopathic pulmonary arterial hypertension (IPAH). Common symptoms of pulmonary hypertension include shortness of breath, fatigue, chest pain, dizziness, swelling in the ankles and legs, and a bluish color to the lips or skin. Symptoms often worsen over time and can significantly affect the quality of life. Pulmonary hypertension can affect individuals of all ages, including children, but it is most commonly diagnosed in adults. It occurs globally, with no specific geographic distribution, though its prevalence varies depending on the underlying cause and access to healthcare. While there is no cure for pulmonary hypertension, current treatments aim to manage symptoms and improve quality of life. Options include medications (vasodilators, blood thinners, diuretics), oxygen therapy, lifestyle changes, and in severe cases, lung transplantation. Early diagnosis and management are crucial for better outcomes.

Prader-Willi Syndrome 普瑞德威利症候群

Prader-Willi Syndrome (PWS) is a complex genetic disorder caused by the loss of function of specific genes on chromosome 15. It usually occurs due to a spontaneous genetic error and is rarely inherited. PWS affects many parts of the body and is primarily characterized by two stages: poor muscle tone (hypotonia), feeding difficulties, and growth delays in infancy, followed by an insatiable appetite leading to chronic overeating (hyperphagia) and obesity in childhood. Besides hyperphagia, PWS symptoms include intellectual disabilities, behavioral problems, short stature, low muscle tone, and distinctive facial features. Other complications may involve sleep disturbances, hormonal deficiencies, and a high risk of obesity-related health problems like type 2 diabetes. PWS affects males and females equally and occurs in all ethnic groups worldwide, with an estimated prevalence of 1 in 10,000 to 30,000 individuals. While there is no cure, treatment focuses on managing symptoms and improving quality of life. Early intervention, including hormone therapy (growth hormone), strict dietary supervision, behavioral therapy, and physical therapy, can help manage the condition. Addressing complications related to obesity and metabolic issues is crucial for improving outcomes.
